The Four Kinds of Knowing

Vervaeke argues that modern culture has collapsed all forms of knowing into a single kind — propositional knowing (having justified true beliefs) — and that this collapse is itself a driver of the meaning crisis. Against this reduction, he distinguishes four irreducible kinds: propositional (knowing-that), procedural (knowing-how), perspectival (knowing what it is like), and participatory (knowing through being-in-relation). These form a hierarchy of asymmetric dependence: propositional depends on procedural, procedural on perspectival, perspectival on participatory — but none reduces to another. Recovering the non-propositional kinds of knowing is not a philosophical curiosity but a practical necessity: transformation, wisdom, and religio all operate at the procedural, perspectival, and participatory levels. This concept runs as connective tissue from Episode 1 through the wisdom discussions in Episodes 40–46.

Misconception branches

If: Propositional knowing is the most complete or foundational kind of knowing.
Then: Vervaeke argues propositional knowing is actually the most derivative — it depends on procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing beneath it, not vice versa.

Then: The propositional and procedural/perspectival/participatory are irreducibly different; knowing about mindfulness does not substitute for practicing it.

Misconception branches

If: Participatory knowing is the lowest or most primitive kind — the one we should aim to transcend.
Then: In Vervaeke's hierarchy of dependence, participatory knowing is the most foundational — the highest in the dependency chain — not the lowest in value; all other kinds of knowing depend on it.
If: Learning propositions about participatory knowing cultivates it.
Then: Recovery of participatory knowing requires appropriate practice within an ecology of psycho-technologies; propositional knowledge about it cannot substitute for enacting it.
