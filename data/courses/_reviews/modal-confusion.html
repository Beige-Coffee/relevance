Mini-course · 2 modules

Modal Confusion

After this course, students will be able to articulate why Erich Fromm's distinction between having-needs and being-needs reveals a structural fault-line in modern consumer culture, and why attempting to satisfy being-mode needs through having-mode strategies generates a self-compounding spiral of frustration — what Vervaeke calls modal confusion. Students will understand how the palace myth in the Siddhartha story enacts, not just describes, this confusion, and how mindfulness (sati) is presented as a counteractive response. This concept sits at the intersection of the series' existential and cognitive-science threads: it connects Aristotle's agent-arena coupling to Buddhist diagnosis to the contemporary therapeutic interest in modes of engagement rather than accumulation of beliefs. Understanding modal confusion is essential for grasping why parasitic processing is so tenacious.

Prerequisites: having-mode-being-mode

Module 1

Having-Mode vs Being-Mode: Two Kinds of Need

Distinguish having-needs from being-needs and explain why each requires a different existential orientation.

Exposition

Episode 7 · Aristotle's World View and Erich Fromm
“Fromm's point is we get mixed up. We try to satisfy our being needs within the having mode. We suffer from 'Modal Confusion'.”
Core definition; introduced via the Siddhartha-and-the-palace narrative.
Episode 7 · Aristotle's World View and Erich Fromm
“This process by which you are co-identifying agency and arena so that they fit together and make sense of each other and you get a coherent and functioning world view... That's your existential mode.”
Grounding the having/being distinction in the agent-arena structure.

Misconception branches

If: The having mode is inherently bad or selfish.
Then: Vervaeke is explicit: the having mode is not wrong — you need water, food, shelter. The problem arises only when being-mode needs are pursued through having-mode strategies.
If: Modal confusion is just about materialism or greed.
Then: Modal confusion is a structural existential misalignment, not a moral failing. It can occur in entirely non-material domains, including pursuing meaningful relationships through control and manipulation.

Module 2

The Spiral of Modal Confusion

Explain how modal confusion self-compounds and why straightforward willpower or belief-change cannot break it.

Exposition

Episode 38 · Agape and 4E Cognitive Science
“We can address modal confusion by the cultivation of Sati.”
Vervaeke's summary response — sati (mindfulness) as the counteractive practice.
Episode 7 · Aristotle's World View and Erich Fromm
“Fromm's point is we get mixed up. We try to satisfy our being needs within the having mode. We suffer from 'Modal Confusion'.”
The self-reinforcing character: the more you pursue, the less satisfied, the more you pursue.

Misconception branches

If: Modal confusion is a cognitive error you can fix by understanding it intellectually.
Then: Vervaeke's point is that modal confusion is an existential misalignment between mode and need — it operates at the level of participatory and perspectival knowing, not propositional belief, so it requires practice-based cultivation to address.
