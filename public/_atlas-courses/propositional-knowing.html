Propositional Knowing

This course examines what Vervaeke calls propositional knowing — the form of knowledge captured in true or false beliefs and statements — and why the modern reduction of all knowing to this single mode is both historically produced and philosophically problematic. Students will be able to articulate how propositional knowing differs from procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing, explain why it is the most derivative of the four kinds rather than the most foundational, and identify the cultural and intellectual forces that produced its contemporary dominance. Understanding propositional knowing is the gateway to grasping why the meaning crisis cannot be resolved by acquiring better information alone, and why transformation — not merely new beliefs — is required for genuine wisdom.

Misconception branches

If: Propositional knowing is the highest or most foundational kind of knowing.
Then: Vervaeke argues the opposite: propositional knowing is the most derivative, depending on procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing beneath it — it presupposes rather than generates the other three.
If: Having the right beliefs about a practice (such as mindfulness or virtue) is sufficient for genuine knowledge of it.
Then: Propositional and procedural/perspectival/participatory knowing are irreducibly different: one can know all the correct propositions about mindfulness and still have none of the skill, perspective, or participatory contact that actual practice produces.

Misconception branches

If: The reduction of all knowing to propositional knowing is a neutral epistemic choice.
Then: Vervaeke argues it is a historically produced outcome of the meaning crisis itself — the same forces that generated the crisis also generated the epistemic reduction, making propositional dominance a symptom as much as a cause.
If: Propositional knowing is useless or unimportant in Vervaeke's framework.
Then: Propositional knowing is genuinely valuable — Vervaeke's critique is not that it should be discarded but that it must be grounded in and responsive to the lower-order knowings rather than attempting to replace them.
