Transframing

Transframing is Vervaeke's coinage for a bi-directional, participatory transformation that simultaneously reframes how one sees the world and reframes how one sees oneself — a systematic restructuring of the whole framing process, not merely the reframing of a particular problem. Ordinary insight reframes a problem within a given perspective; transframing transforms the perspective-making capacity itself. Iris Murdoch's mother-in-law example is canonical: she does not merely see her daughter-in-law differently but becomes a different kind of seer in the process. Transframing is the cognitive signature of wisdom and of genuine sacred symbolic engagement. Students will learn to distinguish transframing from reframing, understand the conditions that necessitate it (existential stupefaction), and grasp how gnosis, ritual, and serious play are its primary vehicles. This concept is central to Vervaeke's account of how awakening from the meaning crisis is not just intellectual but existential.

Prerequisites: The Four Kinds of Knowing, The Meaning Crisis

Transframing vs. Reframing

Distinguish transframing (transformation of the whole framing process) from ordinary reframing (finding a new perspective on an existing problem), using Vervaeke's canonical formulation.

Exposition

“It's not a reframing of a particular problem. It's a transformation of the whole framing process: Both ends.”
Episode 40 · Wisdom and Rationality
“it not only is reframing the problem. It is transforming my competence so it is a transframing insight. It is a systematic insight.”
Episode 17 · Gnosis and Existential Inertia
“she's not only reframing how she sees the world, she's reframing how she sees herself and these are happening in a completely interfused manner. This is a Participatory Change.”

Misconception branches

If: Transframing is just a more dramatic version of reframing — seeing a problem from a very different angle.
Then: Transframing is not a change of perspective on an existing problem but a transformation of the capacity that generates perspectives itself; the 'both ends' formulation marks this as a different kind of change.

L.A. Paul's vampire example shows you can't know what you'll lose or gain from a transformative experience until you've gone through it. How does this create a genuine rational impasse that goes beyond ordinary uncertainty?

Misconception branches

If: People who are stuck just need better information or stronger motivation to change.
Then: Existential entrapment is not an information or motivation deficit; it is a cognitive-existential paralysis in which the agent cannot imaginatively inhabit an alternative worldview from within the current one.
If: Transframing is just making up your mind to change — a matter of will.
Then: Transframing cannot be achieved by willpower because it is a participatory transformation that requires the structural conditions of gnosis — it is not a decision but an undergoing.

Misconception branches

If: Religious ritual is fundamentally different from modern practices like therapy or martial arts.
Then: Vervaeke argues that therapy, Tai Chi, martial arts, and role-playing games like Jeepform all constitute serious play in the technical sense — they are ritual by another name, functioning as psycho-technologies for transframing.
If: Transframing is a one-time breakthrough event — once you transframe, you are done.
Then: The systematic transframing of wisdom is an ongoing developmental process of expanding competence, not a single dramatic event; wisdom cultivates the capacity for transframing, not a permanent final transformation.
